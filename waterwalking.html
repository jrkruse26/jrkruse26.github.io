Walk on Water Project

Project Overview

Project goal: To design and test a prototype of a system which will allow a person to safely walk across a body of water. The system is intended to be marketed for sale.

Team Size: 3 students

Team mission statement: To work as a team in order to design and develop products that adhere to environmental concerns and financial restraints.

Limitations:

  • No batteries
  • No liquid fuel
  • No styrofoam
  • Nothing may come into contact with the bottom or sides of the pool
  • Cost must be under $80

Timeline:

  • Project Assigned: January 18th, 2017
  • Intital Testing: Febuary 20th, 2017
  • Final Testing and Grading: Febuary 25th, 2017

Survey

In order to properly understand the wants and needs of the consumers that our project was being marketed towards, a general interest survey was conducted. From the results of our survey we concluded that our customers were lookings for a recreation product that could be used in any body of water, and valued cost and safety the most.

Defining Specifications

After receiving potential customer input, we developed a table of customer specifications that we would attempt to work with in order to best meet the needs of the customers

Customer Opinion Specificaion
Usability
  • Shoes are easy to remove
  • Adjustable shoes
  • Could be worn with or without shoes
  • Rental option
  • Takes under 20 seconds to prepare for use and to remove the shoes
Safety
  • Additional poles for stability
  • Follows a safety manual
Durability
  • Does not fall apart in water
Cost
  • Product sale cost under $100
Appearance
  • Visually appealing to 70% of potential customers surveyed
Design
  • Designed primarily for recreational use

CAD and Prototyping

Initial CAD Drawing

To fully define the specs and measurments of our design, we drew up a CAD drawing of the shoe in solidworks, then created a proper multiview to reference when building the prototype to ensure accuracy.

Prototyping

After defining the specs of our design, we purchased materials and began constructing a prototype to be used in testing. Our prototype used primarily Medium Density Fiberboard, woodglue, and nails, and was then covered in 2 sheets of plastics to prevent water from compromising the structural integrity of the fiberboard. This was the stage of the project where I confirmed that I do not want to be a carpenter.

Testing

Initial Testing

Upon inital testing, we confirmed that our device did float, but we had underestimated the outward force applied by the person standing and moving on the shoe in water, which cause both shoes to spread apart and rotate outwards, not allowing the user to stand on the shoe very long. We also found that some of the plastic was not secure and water and entered the outer area of the shoe, allowing for our design to be potential compromised if it had been left in the water longer.

Retrofitting

After discovering these instabilities in our design, we retrofitted it in the time we had before the final testing. In order to counteract the force pushing the shoes apart, we added 2 pieces of rope on the front and back of the shoes, keeping them together. We also added two outriggers to the outer side of each shoe made of soda bottles to couteract the outwards rotation of the shoes, keeping it stable. To prevent water from compromising the stability of the medium density fiberboard, we coated the fiberboard in a sprayable sealant, then more carefully applied the outside plastic with a very liberal use of duct tape. We also added poles on the outside to improve stability.

Final Testing

During our final test we expereinced much better results. The outriggers did their job and prevented the shoes from rotating, and the shoes did not take on water. On our first run we made the mistake of not tightning the rope tight enough and the shoes spread too far apart half way across the pool, causing me to fall. After a quick fix we did another run the with the ropes more secured and the shoes worked perfectly. We were also about to mount our shoes in the middle of the pool with no support aside from the water itself and the floating shoe, proving their ease of use.
